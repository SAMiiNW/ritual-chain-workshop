# Reflection

> "What should be public, what should stay hidden, and what should be decided by
> AI versus by a human in a bounty system?"

I'd split it by what each piece of information is *for*. Anything that defines
how the game is played — the prompt, the scoring criteria, the prize size, and
the timestamps that bound each phase — has to be out in the open, because that is
what lets a contestant decide whether to bother and lets an outsider check the
result was fair. The entries themselves are the opposite: their whole value is
being original, and originality evaporates the moment a rival can read your work
mid-contest, so they belong behind a seal until entry is closed. What can safely
become public afterwards is the cleartext (or, in the encrypted variant, never —
only the ranking). On the judging side, I'd hand the AI the wide and boring part:
skim every entry against the rubric and rank them consistently in a single pass,
which no human wants to do fairly forty times in a row. But the AI shouldn't be
the one moving money, because a model can be talked into the wrong answer by a
slick entry and it can't be held responsible afterwards. So the final call — and
the payout — stays with a human who read the AI's summary and owns the decision.
Public rules, sealed content, AI for triage, a person on the trigger.
